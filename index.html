Early Childhood Caries Prevention

Evidence-informed guidance for supporting lasting behaviour change in the families of infants, toddlers, and preschool-aged children.

Clinicians devote considerable time to educating parents on brushing, diet, bottle use, snacking, and fluoride. In the context of early childhood caries, however, information alone rarely produces lasting behaviour change.

A more effective approach shifts the conversation from prescribing instructions to helping each family identify a single, realistic change they are prepared to make.

Clinical Reference

A randomized controlled trial published by the American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry reported that a motivational interviewing intervention promoted preventive behaviours among mothers of young children at high caries risk.

Read the AAPD article · AAPD ECC guidance

Guiding the parent conversation

Rather than presenting a comprehensive list of instructions, targeted questions can help identify the underlying barrier:

“What part of the routine feels hardest right now?”

“What is one change you think would be realistic this week?”

“What feels like the most realistic place to start: brushing, bedtime drink habits, or reducing frequent snacks?”

This fosters a collaborative discussion and allows the parent to define an achievable first step. For many families, the barrier is not a lack of concern. More often it reflects competing demands such as daily routine, time, stress, a child's cooperation, established feeding practices, or uncertainty about which measures matter most.

Prevention Framework

A concise ECC prevention framework

When speaking with parents of infants, toddlers, and preschool-aged children, a short “menu” of options can be helpful.

•  Brush twice daily with fluoridated toothpaste

•  Use only a smear/rice-sized amount for children under 3

•  Use a pea-sized amount for children ages 3 to 6

•  Avoid putting anything sweet in the bottle

•  Focus on stopping nighttime bottle or milk habits first

•  Limit frequent sipping and snacking throughout the day

•  Encourage water between meals

•  Schedule regular preventive visits and fluoride varnish when indicated

The objective is not to overwhelm the parent, but to support a clear and achievable first step.

When to refer

A pediatric dental referral is appropriate when a young child presents with:

✓ White spot or cavitated lesions
✓ Caries before age 3
✓ Multiple affected teeth
✓ Limited cooperation for exam or radiographs
✓ High caries-risk history in siblings

Timely referral can prevent early lesions from progressing to more extensive restorative, sedation, or emergency care.
